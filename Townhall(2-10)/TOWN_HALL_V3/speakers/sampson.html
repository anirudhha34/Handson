What's 75 million years old and brand spanking new? A teenage Utahceratops! Come to the Saroyan, armed with your best dinosaur roar, when Scott Sampson, Research Curator at the Utah Museum of Natural History, steps to the podium. Sampson's research has focused on the ecology and evolution of late Cretaceous dinosaurs and he has conducted fieldwork in a number of countries in Africa. Scott Sampson is a Canadian-Dom paleontologist who received his Ph.D. in 200logy from the University of Toronto. Hs doctoral work focused on two new species of ceratopsids or homed dinosaurs from the Late Cretaceous of Montana as well as the growth and function of certopsid norms and frills. Following graduation in 1003. Sampson spent a year working at the American Museum of Natual History in New York Cry followed by five years as assistant professor of anatomy at the New York College of Osteopathic Medicine on Long Island He armved at the University of Utah accepting a dual position as assistant professor in the Department of Geology and Geophysics and curator of vertebrale paleontology at the Utah Museum of Natural History. His research interests largely revolve around the phylogenetics, tunctional morphology and evolution of Late Cretaceous dinosaurs In addition to his museum and laboratory-based studies, Sampson has conducted palcontological work in Zimbabwe, South Africa, and Madagascar as well as the United States and Canada He was also the on-the-air host for the Discovery Channers Dinosaur Planet and recently completed a book, Dinosaur Odyssey: Fossi Threads in the Web of Life, which is one of the most comprehensive surveys of dinosaurs and their worlds to date.
